Literature Survey

Attention Deficit Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD) affects 2- 7 percent of children worldwide, leading to academic, social, and emotional challenges. Timely identification is crucial for effective treatment. However, in Sri Lanka, lack of awareness hinders therapy access. Existing solutions have gaps in iden- tifying ADHD [1]. Innovations are needed to bridge this gap and improve accessibility. ADHD’s global prevalence ranges from 5-10 percent in school-aged children. The disorder significantly impacts aca- demics, social interactions, and overall well-being [2]. Un- treated ADHD results in educational difficulties, low self- esteem, and relationship struggles. Traditional ADHD detection methods in Sri Lanka include activities like painting and puzzles, but these lack accuracy and reliability. Limited resources and tailored therapy hinder interventions. Machine learning shows promise in ADHD detection. Abbas et al. used eye-tracking and neural networks to achieve high accuracy. Game-based interventions offer effective ADHD assessment and treatment. They engage users, improve cognitive skills, and target specific symptoms [3] [4].

Implementing a game-based mobile app for ADHD in Sri Lanka faces challenges like low awareness, resource scarcity, and technological barriers [5]. However, the prevalence of mobile devices presents an opportunity to raise awareness and enhance access. Such an app can be cost-effective, scalable, and user-friendly. In conclusion, early ADHD detection and treatment are crucial in Sri Lanka. Machine learning and game-based mobile apps hold promise to improve accuracy and accessibility. Future research should validate the app’s effectiveness, address cultural considerations, and ensure lasting benefits for children with ADHD.

Research Gap

Research gaps related to ADHD in Sri Lankan children aged between 6-12

Cultural Impacts on ADHD
Limited research exists on how cultural beliefs, family dynamics, and societal norms in Sri Lanka influence the recognition and handling of ADHD in children aged 6-12.
Healthcare Access Challenges
Understanding the obstacles to accessing healthcare facilities and specialized ADHD resources in Sri Lanka is crucial, focusing on affordability, availability, and cultural acceptability.
Tailored Interventions Efficacy
Limited research exists on how well culturally adapted interventions work for managing ADHD in Sri Lankan children. Investigating their effectiveness is vital for better management.

Research Problem & Solution

Identified Problem

In Sri Lanka, there exists a significant lack of awareness, resources, and accessible therapy services for children with Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD). This dearth of resources presents a critical challenge, necessitating the development of a more reliable and effective mobile solution to identify and treat children with ADHD. Addressing this scarcity is imperative to improve the diagnosis and treatment of ADHD in Sri Lanka.

Proposed Solution

A specialized mobile application designed for children aged 6 to 12 in Sri Lanka aims to accurately identify ADHD symptoms. The app's child-friendly interface focuses on symptoms such as difficulty following directions, lack of concentration, memory challenges, and difficulty responding to commands. It offers tailored treatments and interventions to address these specific symptoms, providing a comprehensive support system for children with ADHD in Sri Lanka.
